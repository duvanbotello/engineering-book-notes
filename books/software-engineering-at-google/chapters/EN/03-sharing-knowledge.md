# 03 - Sharing knowledge

## Core idea

Knowledge sharing is not optional, it is a condition for engineering to scale. When knowledge remains in islands, errors, duplication, and dependency on a few people increase.

## Key concepts

- **Information islands:** they appear when teams or areas do not share resources or practices.
- **Fragmentation costs:** duplication, bias, and unnecessary differences in working methods emerge.
- **Single point of failure:** relying on one person turns any absence into operational risk.
- **All-or-nothing expertise:** without gradual transfer, learning becomes slow and fragile.
- **Group chat channels:** they enable fast questions, collective answers, and visible learning for more people.
- **Searchable records:** when conversations are recorded, knowledge becomes reusable.
- **Open documentation:** allowing anyone to improve docs reduces friction and accelerates small fixes.
- **Knowledge rhythms:** weekly tips and technical newsletters sustain continuous learning across the organization.
- **Readability participation:** reviewing and being reviewed strengthens shared quality standards.
- **Start small:** asking questions and writing things down builds the foundation of a knowledge culture.
- **Psychological safety:** it is the base for people to ask, correct, and share.

## Quotes (short only)

- "Information islands fragment knowledge."
- "Do not hide."
- "Start small, ask questions, and write things down."

## My interpretation

The strongest lesson is that shared knowledge reduces systemic risk. The problem is not only that someone knows a lot, but that others cannot use that knowledge in time. A healthy culture turns doubts into public conversations, documentation into a living asset, and errors into collective learning.

## Practical lessons

- Every useful answer in a public channel prevents repeated future interruptions.
- If documentation cannot be edited easily, it becomes obsolete faster.
- Without psychological safety, people stay silent and the organization learns slower.
- Knowledge sharing requires habit, not only tools.
- Standardizing learning spaces (tips, newsletters, reviews) improves technical consistency.

## Questions

- Which critical knowledge is still trapped in a few people or teams?
- Are our chat channels and documents truly searchable and reusable?
- Do we have regular mechanisms to share small learnings?
- Do people feel safe saying "I don't know" and asking for help?

## Actions

- [ ] Create a weekly "tip of the week" ritual in the team's technical channel.
- [ ] Establish a rule: every recurring solution must end up documented.
- [ ] Allow and encourage open documentation edits, including small corrections.
- [ ] Publish a monthly internal newsletter with engineering and security learnings.
- [ ] Review potential knowledge single points of failure every quarter.
