# 05 - Team Leadership

## Core idea

An engineering team needs active leadership to sustain focus and stability. Effective leadership is not about controlling everything: it is about serving the team with humility, respect, and trust, setting clear direction, and creating conditions for autonomous growth.

## Key concepts

- **Leadership principles:** clear direction, humility, respect, trust, and calm under pressure.
- **Professional boundaries:** trying to be everyone's friend is an anti-pattern; keep healthy boundaries between personal and professional roles.
- **Influence and power:** when a leader influences someone's career, responses can become artificial; use connection spaces (for example, team lunches) without blurring roles.
- **Autonomy over control:** do not treat the team like children; avoid micromanagement unless there is a clear need.
- **Ego and credibility:** you do not need all the answers; pretending you do reduces respect. It is better to admit limits, accept criticism, and avoid territorial behavior.
- **Leadership as facilitation:** help through questions, act as a catalyst, and remove blockers instead of personally solving everything.
- **Effective mentorship:** combine team-process experience, a teaching mindset, and the ability to calibrate the level of help.
- **Team management:** define explicit goals and milestones, ensure alignment, delegate without disengaging, protect the team from chaos, and make good work visible.
- **Human motivation:** treat each person as an individual and design work around autonomy, mastery, and purpose.

## Quotes (short only)

- "A ship without a captain is just a floating waiting room."
- "The best leaders work to serve their team using humility, respect, and trust."
- "Resist the temptation to manage."
- "You do not need all the answers, but you should know who knows."
- "Is there anything I can help you with?"

## My interpretation

The strongest message in this chapter is that technical leadership matures when focus shifts from individual protagonism to sustainable team performance. A good leader does not centralize decisions out of ego: they create clarity, care for the human context, support with judgment, and enable others to succeed. The outcome is a more autonomous, honest, and resilient team.

## Practical lessons

- Defining concrete goals and milestones prevents ambiguous performance conversations.
- Frequent and early follow-up works better than late corrections.
- Useful mentorship guides through questions and calibration, not over-intervention.
- Being honest about what you do not know strengthens leadership credibility.
- Direct, empathetic feedback is more effective than mechanical techniques such as the "feedback sandwich."
- Autonomy needs active support: delegating does not mean disengaging.
- Recognizing achievements and making good work visible improves motivation and commitment.

## Questions

- Am I leading to serve the team or to control execution?
- Am I keeping clear professional boundaries without losing human closeness?
- Are my success and failure expectations truly measurable through milestones?
- Am I helping through questions that build judgment, or solving everything myself?
- Am I being direct and empathetic in feedback, without artificial framing?
- Do I understand what motivates each team member beyond external incentives?

## Actions

- [ ] Define explicit goals and milestones for key sprint tasks.
- [ ] In each 1:1, ask: "Is there anything I can help you with?"
- [ ] Schedule weekly follow-up with any member who needs close support.
- [ ] Practice direct and empathetic feedback, avoiding the "feedback sandwich."
- [ ] Delegate one meaningful responsibility and set clear checkpoints without micromanaging.
- [ ] Review hiring practices to prioritize people who raise the team's level.
- [ ] Give weekly visibility to team achievements in a shared channel or forum.
- [ ] Evaluate leadership decisions using humility, respect, and trust as criteria.
