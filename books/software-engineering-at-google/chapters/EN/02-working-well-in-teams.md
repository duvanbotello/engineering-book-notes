# 02 - Working well in teams

## Core idea

Software development is a team effort. Working visibly, with humility, respect, and trust, lowers risk, improves decisions, and accelerates collective learning.

## Key concepts

- **Do not hide:** many eyes help keep the project relevant and correct direction earlier.
- **Risk of working alone:** isolation is usually more costly than exposing an imperfect idea.
- **Three pillars of social interaction:** humility, respect, and trust sustain technical collaboration.
- **Richer working relationships:** better relationships increase willingness to support teammates when needed.
- **Failure as learning:** a blameless post-mortem culture turns errors into real improvements.
- **Document to improve:** each review should capture what failed, what was learned, and what will change.
- **Technical honesty:** saying "I don't know" early prevents weak decisions and opens learning.

## Quotes (short only)

- "Software development is a team effort."
- "Do not hide."
- "Sometimes the best thing you can do is say: I don't know."

## My interpretation

This chapter shows that good collaboration is not an optional soft skill: it is an engineering practice that protects time, quality, and product direction. The real risk is not showing uncertainty; it is investing heavily in the wrong direction due to lack of challenge.

## Practical lessons

- Sharing progress early avoids waste on misaligned solutions.
- Asking for review is not weakness; it is risk control.
- Technical humility improves shared decision quality.
- Blameless post-mortems only work when they end in concrete changes.
- Saying "I don't know" honestly strengthens team trust.

## Questions

- Am I sharing work early enough, or waiting too long for feedback?
- Does my team practice humility, respect, and trust explicitly?
- Do our retrospectives document concrete changes, or only explain what happened?
- Am I avoiding "I don't know" because of ego or fear?

## Actions

- [ ] Share intermediate progress for review before closing implementation.
- [ ] Include three minimum points in every post-mortem: failure, learning, and applied change.
- [ ] In 1:1s, check for signs of low trust or low psychological safety.
- [ ] Practice explicit "I don't know" responses when technical context is missing.
- [ ] Recognize others' contributions to reinforce respect and collaboration.
